    Can you sell a house before the divorce is finalised?

    Yes. In most cases the family home forms part of the financial settlement, so there's no need to wait for the final order before you sell. Many separating couples choose to sell early so they can each move on, free up their equity and draw a clear line under the relationship.

    Before agreeing to sell, it's important that both parties understand how the proceeds will be split — what the mortgage and any secured debts will take, and how the remaining equity will be divided. We always recommend that each of you takes independent legal advice so the arrangement is fair and properly documented. Our role is simply to make the sale itself fast, certain and private.

    What happens to a house during divorce?

    Typically there are three options:

    1. Sell and split the proceeds

    Often the cleanest break. The property is sold, the outstanding mortgage is repaid and the costs of sale are deducted. The remaining equity is then split between you in line with your financial settlement, leaving you both free to move forward independently. A fast cash sale makes this route simple and certain.

    2. One partner buys the other out

    One of you keeps the home and buys out the other's share. This usually means getting a valuation, calculating the equity, agreeing a figure and transferring ownership and the mortgage into a single name. The remaining owner will need to satisfy the lender that they can carry the mortgage alone — which isn't always possible.

    3. Continue owning jointly

    Some couples choose to keep the property jointly for a time — commonly where children are involved, or where the market is poor and selling now wouldn't be sensible. It can work, but joint ownership after separation can also create future complications, so clear legal agreements are essential if you take this route.

    Do both spouses need to agree to sell?

    For a property that is jointly owned, the answer is usually yes — both owners normally need to agree before it can be sold. When you're on the same page, the process is straightforward and a sale can move quickly.

    If one party refuses to sell, things become more complex. In some cases legal intervention may be needed, for example a court order, to move matters forward. The good news is that disputes like this can often be avoided. Taking early legal advice and keeping communication open tends to lead to a far smoother outcome for everyone involved.
