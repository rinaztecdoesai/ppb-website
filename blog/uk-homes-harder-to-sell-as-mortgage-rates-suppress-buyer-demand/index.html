Homes Are Taking Longer to Sell as High Mortgage Rates Shrink Buyer Demand

High mortgage rates are making UK homes harder to sell. See when a fast cash house sale or quick cash buyer may help sellers move on with certainty.

Three in five homes listed for sale in the UK since January are still on the market, as high mortgage rates continue to limit what buyers can afford.

Property portal Zoopla says agreed sales are running below last year’s levels, with some sellers now facing a difficult choice: reduce the asking price, wait longer, or consider a faster route to sale.

The slowdown is not hitting every part of the market equally. Wales and the East Midlands have seen sharper falls in agreed sales, while northern England and Scotland have held up better because average prices , and therefore monthly mortgage costs , are lower.

For sellers, the message is becoming clear: homes are still selling, but buyers are more cautious, lenders are stricter, and overpriced properties are sitting for longer.

Why Homes Are Harder to Sell Right Now

The biggest pressure on the housing market is affordability.

Mortgage rates rose sharply in spring, pushing monthly repayments higher for buyers who were already stretched by inflation, rent, and wider living costs.

Moneyfacts data showed the average two-year fixed mortgage rate rising from 4.83% at the start of March to a peak of 5.90% in April, before easing back. That increase added around £125 a month to a typical mortgage at the peak.

In London, the impact was even sharper. First-time buyers faced an estimated £232 extra per month at the height of the rate spike.

That kind of jump changes buyer behaviour quickly.

Some buyers pause their search. Some lower their budget. Others make offers only if sellers are willing to negotiate. The result is a market where listings are available, but serious proceedable buyers are harder to find.

The Gap Between Asking Prices and Realistic Offers

A slower market exposes overpricing.

When demand is strong, sellers can sometimes test the market with an ambitious asking price. When borrowing costs rise, buyers become less forgiving.

Zoopla has warned that many unsold homes are not failing because there are no buyers at all, but because the price does not match what buyers can now afford.

That means sellers need to look beyond asking prices on competing listings. The better benchmark is recent agreed sales in the same area, for similar properties, in similar condition.

If a home has had viewings but no offers, the market may be saying the price is too high.

If it has had little interest at all, the issue may be price, presentation, location, property type, or a smaller pool of buyers for that segment.

Flats and First-Time Buyer Homes Are Under More Pressure

First-time buyers are usually the most exposed to mortgage rate rises because they often borrow at higher loan-to-value levels.

That pressure is showing up in the type of homes left unsold.

One and two-bedroom flats have been harder to shift than larger family homes. Zoopla reported that around two-thirds of one and two-bedroom flats listed this year had not yet found a buyer.

Two and three-bedroom houses have performed better, partly because they appeal to a wider group of movers and families with more equity.

This matters for sellers because the national market headline does not tell the full story.

A well-priced family home in a lower-cost region may still attract strong interest. A flat in a more expensive city, aimed at first-time buyers, may need sharper pricing or more flexibility.

Where the Slowdown Is Most Visible

The UK housing market is not moving at one speed.

Zoopla’s figures show agreed sales down around 7% compared with last year, but the regional picture varies.

Wales has seen agreed sales fall by around 12%, while the East Midlands is down around 11%. London remains under affordability pressure, with high prices magnifying the effect of mortgage rate changes.

Northern England and Scotland have been more resilient. In these areas, lower average house prices mean monthly mortgage payments have not risen as dramatically in cash terms.

For sellers, that means local evidence matters more than national averages.

Before listing, or before deciding whether to cut the price, compare your home with similar properties that have actually sold nearby , not just homes still sitting online.

Are Mortgage Rates Starting to Improve?

There are signs of more competition among lenders.

After the April spike, average mortgage rates began to ease as lenders adjusted pricing and competed for borrowers. That is positive for buyers, but it does not instantly restore demand.

A small fall in mortgage rates can take time to feed through into buyer confidence. Some people will wait for clearer signs that rates are falling. Others may still fail affordability checks even if headline rates improve slightly.

The Bank of England also reported mortgage approvals falling to a two-and-a-half-year low in May, showing how quickly higher rates affected the number of buyers able to proceed.

So while the market may improve if mortgage rates continue to fall, sellers cannot assume demand will bounce back immediately.

What Sellers Should Do If Their Home Is Not Moving

If your property has been on the market for weeks with little interest, start with three questions.

First, is the asking price based on recent sold prices or optimistic listings?

Second, does the property compete well on presentation, photos, condition, and energy efficiency?

Third, is your target buyer likely to be affected by higher mortgage costs?

If the home is aimed at first-time buyers, investors, or buyers needing high borrowing, price sensitivity will be higher.

A realistic adjustment early can be more effective than waiting months and reducing later. Properties that sit online for too long can become stale, and buyers may assume there is something wrong with them.

Sellers should also factor in the cost of waiting. Mortgage payments, council tax, insurance, maintenance, and lost onward-purchase opportunities all add up.

Sometimes holding out for a higher price makes sense. Sometimes certainty is worth more.

The Bottom Line

High mortgage rates have not stopped the UK housing market, but they have changed it.

Buyers are more cautious. First-time buyer demand is weaker. Flats are taking longer to sell. Overpriced homes are sitting. Regional differences are becoming more important.

For sellers, the answer is not panic. It is realism.

Price against current demand, not last year’s expectations. Watch what is actually selling near you. Be prepared to negotiate. And if time matters more than squeezing out the highest possible price, compare the open market with a direct cash sale.
