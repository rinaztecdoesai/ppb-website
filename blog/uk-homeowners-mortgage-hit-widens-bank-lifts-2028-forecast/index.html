One Million More UK Homeowners Face Higher Mortgage Bills

More UK homeowners face higher mortgage bills. See when a quick cash house sale or fast cash buyer may help owners avoid payment pressure.

More than five million UK homeowners are expected to see their monthly mortgage repayments rise by the end of 2028, according to the Bank of England.

The latest forecast is higher than the Bank expected in December, when around four million households were projected to face bigger mortgage bills.

The change reflects a tougher interest rate outlook after the Iran war pushed up energy costs, inflation expectations, and borrowing costs across global markets.

For many homeowners, the increase will not be as severe as the payment shocks seen after 2022. Even so, the figures show that the pressure from higher mortgage rates is still working its way through household finances.

The hardest hit group will be owners coming off very cheap fixed rate deals. The Bank said around 750,000 homeowners currently paying less than 3% interest are due to refinance this year, with an average monthly increase of about £170.

Why More Homeowners Face Higher Mortgage Bills

Most UK mortgage customers are on fixed rate deals. That means their monthly payment stays the same until the deal ends, usually after two or five years.

This protects borrowers from immediate rate rises, but it also delays the impact. When the fixed period ends, the homeowner has to choose a new product at current market rates.

Many people who fixed before rates rose are still paying far less than today’s available deals. As those mortgages expire, the higher cost of borrowing moves from financial markets into monthly household budgets.

The Bank of England now expects just over five million homeowners to face higher repayments by the end of 2028. That is one million more than it expected in its previous forecast.

The key point is timing. The mortgage shock has not disappeared. It has been spread across several years because fixed rate deals expire at different times.

How Much Could Mortgage Payments Rise?

The Bank said a typical owner occupier rolling off a fixed rate in the next two years is likely to see monthly repayments rise by about £45.

That is smaller than the average increase of about £120 faced by borrowers who refinanced between late 2022 and late 2024.

However, averages can hide much sharper pressure for certain households.

Homeowners still on very low rates face the biggest jump. Around 750,000 borrowers paying less than 3% are expected to refinance this year. The Bank estimates that group could see an average increase of about £170 a month.

For a household already dealing with food, energy, council tax, and insurance costs, an extra £170 a month can force difficult choices.

Some homeowners will cut discretionary spending. Some will delay moving. Others may extend their mortgage term, use savings, or consider selling if the new payment is no longer affordable.

Why Fixed Rate Borrowers Are Still Feeling the Shock

Fixed rate mortgages helped millions of borrowers avoid immediate increases when interest rates rose.

But fixed rates do not remove the risk. They postpone it until the end of the deal.

More than eight in 10 UK mortgage customers have fixed rate products, so the impact of higher rates arrives in waves. Every month, another group of homeowners reaches the end of an older deal and faces the current market.

Borrowers on two year fixed deals are affected more quickly. Borrowers who took five year deals before rates rose may only now be approaching their first major refinancing decision.

For some, the new deal may be close to their existing rate. For others, especially those who fixed below 2% or 3%, the jump can be a major hit to disposable income.

This is why the Bank’s forecast matters. It shows that the mortgage pressure is not just a past event. It remains a live issue for homeowners, lenders, and the wider housing market.

How the Iran War Changed the Interest Rate Outlook

The Bank linked the higher forecast to the economic impact of the Iran war.

The conflict disrupted expectations for energy supply, including concerns around the Strait of Hormuz, which is a key route for global oil and gas shipments.

Higher energy costs can feed into inflation. When inflation is expected to stay higher for longer, markets often expect central banks to keep interest rates higher as well.

That affects mortgage pricing because lenders use market interest rate expectations when setting fixed rate deals.

Moneyfacts data showed the average two year fixed mortgage rate rising from 4.83% at the start of March to 5.90% on 12 April. It later eased to around 5.49%, but the spike changed expectations for many borrowers who had hoped refinancing costs would fall sooner.

For homeowners waiting for cheaper mortgage deals, the message is frustrating. Rates may ease, but the path down is uncertain, and global events can quickly change lender pricing.

What This Means for Homeowners and Sellers

Higher mortgage bills affect more than monthly budgets. They also influence the housing market.

If homeowners expect a large refinancing increase, some will delay moving because taking on a new mortgage feels too expensive.

Others may decide to sell before their new rate becomes unaffordable. That can increase supply in some areas, especially where owners are already stretched.

Buyers are affected too. Higher mortgage rates reduce how much they can borrow, which can lead to lower offers and longer selling times.

This is one reason pricing has become so important. Sellers who base their asking price on the stronger market of previous years may struggle to attract proceedable buyers.

In a market shaped by mortgage affordability, the most successful sellers are usually those who price against recent completed sales, not hopeful listings still sitting online.

How to Prepare Before Your Mortgage Deal Ends

Homeowners approaching the end of a fixed rate should start preparing early.

Check when your current deal expires and ask your lender how soon you can reserve a new product. Many borrowers can secure a rate several months before the existing deal ends, while still switching if a better offer appears later.

Compare the full cost, not just the headline rate. Arrangement fees, valuation fees, term length, and early repayment charges can change the real cost of a mortgage deal.

It is also worth testing your budget against a higher monthly payment before it arrives. If your payment could rise by £100, £170, or more, practise setting that money aside for a few months. This gives you a clearer view of what is affordable.

If the new payment looks difficult, speak to your lender before you miss anything. Options may include extending the term, switching product, moving temporarily to interest only, or reviewing support under the Mortgage Charter.

Independent mortgage advice can also help, especially if your income, credit profile, or property value has changed since your last deal.
