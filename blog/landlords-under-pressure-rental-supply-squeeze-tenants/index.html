Landlords Under Pressure as Rental Supply Squeeze Raises Risk for Tenants

UK landlords face higher mortgage costs, tax pressure and rental reform in 2026. See when a fast cash sale or quick cash buyer may help landlords exit.

More landlords are weighing up whether to sell in 2026 as higher mortgage costs, tax pressure, and new rental rules continue to reshape the UK private rented sector.

The pressure comes even as rent growth has started to slow.

Office for National Statistics figures show average UK private rents rose by 3.3% in the 12 months to May 2026, reaching £1,383 a month. That was down from 3.5% the previous month and continues the wider cooling seen since late 2024.

But slower rent growth does not mean the rental market has become easy for tenants.

Zoopla has reported that there are still far fewer homes available to rent than before the pandemic, while rents remain hundreds of pounds higher than they were a few years ago. For renters, that means competition can still be intense, especially in towns and cities where supply is thin.

For landlords, the calculation is becoming more difficult.

Mortgage payments remain higher than many investors were used to, the stamp duty surcharge on additional homes has made expansion more expensive, and regulation is adding extra cost and uncertainty.

The result is a market where some landlords are holding on, some are raising rents where they can, and others are looking for a fast sale before costs rise further.

Why Landlords Are Still Under Pressure in 2026

The buy to let market has not collapsed, but it has become harder to make the numbers work.

Landlords who bought when mortgage rates were lower are now refinancing in a very different market. Even where rates have eased from their recent peaks, monthly payments can still be much higher than on older fixed deals.

That matters because landlords are judged by lenders on rental income as well as personal finances.

If the rent no longer covers the lender’s stress test, a landlord may struggle to refinance, borrow more, or buy another rental property. Some may need to put in extra cash. Others may decide the investment is no longer worth the risk.

For smaller landlords, the pressure can be sharper.

A landlord with one or two properties has less room to absorb higher mortgage costs, repair bills, void periods, licensing costs, and tax changes. If a tenant leaves or a property needs work, the financial strain can build quickly.

What Falling Rent Growth Really Means for Tenants

Rent growth slowing is good news for tenants, but it does not mean rents are falling.

ONS data shows average UK private rents reached £1,383 a month in May 2026. In England, the average was £1,442. Wales stood at £836, while Scotland reached £1,009.

The North East had the highest annual rent inflation in England at 5.9%, while London had the lowest at 2.0%.

That shows how uneven the market has become.

In some areas, rents are still rising faster than wages. In others, tenants have reached an affordability limit, which means landlords cannot keep increasing rents without risking longer voids or arrears.

Even where rent rises slow, tenants can still struggle if there are too few homes available.

A family looking for a three bedroom house, or a single renter trying to find an affordable flat near work, may still face queues at viewings and pressure to move quickly.

Why Buy To Let Lending Is Still Difficult

Mortgage rates remain one of the biggest challenges for landlords in 2026.

A buy to let mortgage is usually assessed differently from a normal residential mortgage. Lenders often want the rent to cover the mortgage payment by a set margin, especially when interest rates are higher.

That means a property that worked as an investment five years ago may fail today’s affordability test.

Some landlords are also facing higher fees when they remortgage. A lower headline rate can still be expensive if it comes with a large arrangement fee, especially for landlords with smaller portfolios or lower yielding properties.

This has made new investment more cautious.

Landlords are more likely to look for properties with stronger yields, lower purchase prices, and clearer rental demand. That often favours parts of northern England and the Midlands, where entry prices can be lower and rent compared with property value may be stronger.

In expensive areas, especially where yields are thin, more landlords may decide to sell rather than refinance.

The Impact of Tax and Regulation on Landlords

Tax and regulation are also shaping landlord decisions.

The stamp duty surcharge on additional homes makes buying another rental property more expensive. For a landlord buying at £300,000, the extra tax can be a major upfront cost before repairs, mortgage fees, and letting costs are even considered.

Many landlords are also dealing with the long term effect of mortgage interest tax relief changes, which reduced the benefit of offsetting finance costs for personally owned rental property.

On top of that, new rental rules are changing how landlords think about risk.

The Renters’ Rights changes are designed to give tenants more security, but some landlords worry about the practical impact on possession, rent increases, compliance, and management costs.

For professional landlords with strong systems, the changes may be manageable.

For accidental landlords or owners with one property, the extra administration can be enough to make selling look more attractive.

What This Means for Renters Looking for a Home

For renters, the risk is simple: fewer landlords can mean fewer homes to rent.

If landlords sell to owner occupiers, the property may leave the rental market entirely. That can reduce choice for tenants, especially in areas where supply is already tight.

A fall in landlord lending can also mean fewer new rental homes being added.

That does not automatically push rents up everywhere, because affordability still limits what tenants can pay. But it can keep competition high, particularly for well priced homes in good locations.

Tenants looking for a new home in 2026 should prepare early.

Have payslips, references, identification, and deposit funds ready before viewings. Register with more than one letting agent and widen the search area if possible. Be clear on your budget before offering, because over stretching can create problems later.

Good preparation will not fix the supply shortage, but it can help renters move faster when the right property appears.

Why Some Landlords May Decide To Sell

For landlords, 2026 is a decision year.

Some will keep their properties and wait for mortgage rates to fall further. Some will move properties into limited companies, improve energy efficiency, or focus on higher yielding areas.

Others will sell.

The decision often comes down to cash flow.

If rent no longer covers the mortgage, tax, insurance, repairs, and management costs, the property may stop working as an investment. If a landlord also faces a large repair bill, a problem tenant, a short lease, or a remortgage deadline, selling can become the practical choice.

The open market may still be the best option for landlords who have time, a vacant property, and a home in good condition.

But not every landlord has that flexibility.

A tenanted property can be harder to sell to a normal buyer. A property needing repairs can fail surveys or lead to price reductions. A buyer relying on a mortgage can pull out if lending becomes difficult.

That is why some landlords look for a quicker and more certain route.

The Bottom Line

The rental market in 2026 is still under pressure.

Rent growth is slowing, but rents remain high. Tenants still face limited choice in many areas. Landlords are still dealing with higher mortgage costs, tax pressure, and regulatory change.

That mix creates a difficult balance.

If too many landlords sell, rental supply can tighten further. If landlords stay but costs keep rising, tenants may face continued pressure on rents.

For landlords, the right decision depends on the numbers.

If the property still produces reliable income and can handle future costs, holding may make sense. If the mortgage, tax, repairs, or regulation now outweigh the return, selling may be the cleaner option.
