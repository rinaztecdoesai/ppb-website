Selling a House With Sitting Tenants to a Cash Buyer: Guide

Selling a house with sitting tenants to a cash buyer is legal, fast and avoids eviction. Learn how it works, the typical price discount and what to expect.

A specialist buyer purchases the property with the tenant in situ, the existing tenancy transfers on completion, and you avoid eviction, agent fees, and months of waiting. It is often the fastest exit for a buy to let landlord who no longer wants the hassle.

Selling a House With Sitting Tenants to a Cash Buyer: How It Works

Can you sell a property with sitting tenants?

Yes. You can sell a property with sitting tenants in England and Wales, and the buyer must honour the existing tenancy agreement. Most mortgage-funded buyers walk away because lenders usually require vacant possession, which is why the cash buyer market exists for tenanted homes. The sale does not need the tenant's consent, though you must give written notice that the landlord is changing.

With an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST), the new owner steps into your shoes as landlord on the same terms and dates. Cash buyers actively want sitting tenants because rent arrives from day one of completion.

How much does a sitting tenant devalue a house?

A sitting tenant typically reduces a property's market value by 20–25% compared with the vacant possession price. The exact discount depends on the tenant's behaviour, rent level, lease length remaining, and property type.

Cash buyers price below market because they absorb tenant risk, take on management responsibilities, and cannot easily reposition the asset. A direct house cash buyer such as Prime Property Buyers applies a single discount band based on the property, not on how pressed you appear to sell, so the offer you receive is the offer you keep.

Can I sell my house to a cash buyer?

Yes, any homeowner in England can sell their house to a cash buyer regardless of condition, situation, or occupancy. Prime Property Buyers uses its own funds (no investor chains or broker delays), issues a fixed offer that does not drop after survey, and covers your legal fees in full.

Sales complete in as little as 5 days, with the same buyer contact from first call to completion. There are no exclusivity contracts, so you stay free to compare alternatives until exchange.

Can my tenant pay me in cash?

Legally, no. Residential rent in England must be paid by a traceable method such as bank transfer, standing order, or cheque. Accepting cash rent can breach the terms of an AST, expose you to HMRC scrutiny, and complicate a sale because most cash buyers need a clean paper trail of rental income. If your tenant is paying cash, switch to a bank transfer before listing and keep dated statements ready for the buyer's solicitor.

From a sale perspective, how the tenant pays you does not change the cash buyer's offer, but clean, documented rent records help justify a higher valuation.

Can You Sell a House With Sitting Tenants and Should You?

How tenant rights shape your options

Yes, you can sell a house with sitting tenants in England, and in most cases the existing tenancy transfers to the new owner on completion. Your tenant's right to quiet enjoyment means you cannot force them to allow viewings, and under the Renters' Rights Act 2025 you can no longer rely on Section 21 to repossess the property simply because you want to sell. Selling a house with sitting tenants to a cash buyer is a lawful route that protects the tenant's occupation while letting you move on.

Buyers' rights matter too. A buyer purchasing an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST) takes the property subject to the tenancy agreement. They must honour the rent, the deposit protection, and the notice periods you established. If a buyer wants the property empty, they will need a possession route such as Ground 1A, which now requires the tribunal's view of "appropriate" alternative accommodation.

Skip the eviction gamble. A Section 8 Notice remains a slower, court-led process. Most landlords selling tenanted homes use one of three paths:

  • Sell with tenants in situ: the buyer inherits the AST, rent roll, and any protected or periodic tenancy in place.
  • Serve a Section 8 Notice on Ground 1A: pursue possession through the court, which adds months and legal cost.
  • Offer cash for keys: a voluntary surrender agreement, paid only after the tenant returns the keys.

Why most cash buyers prefer tenants in situ

Most cash buyers want tenants in situ because rent from day one offsets their discount and removes the cost of eviction. A direct cash buyer using its own funds can complete in as little as 5 days, with no exclusivity clause, a fixed offer, and legal fees covered, which removes the chain risk that deters lenders from buy to let stock.

Where a buy to let landlord once needed vacant possession to remortgage or exit, a cash buyer takes the tenancy as part of the deal. How Much Does a Sitting Tenant Reduce Your Sale Price?

Realistic discount ranges from our 30 years of buying

A sitting tenant typically reduces a property's sale price by 20–25% compared with its vacant possession value, based on three decades of direct purchases across England. Landlords selling a house with sitting tenants to a cash buyer consistently see offers in that band, though the exact figure depends on the tenancy type, rent level, and tenant profile.

The discount exists for three practical reasons a seller should understand:

  • Buyer pool shrinks. Most retail buyers using a mortgage cannot proceed until the property is empty. That cuts demand to mainly investors and specialist cash buyers.
  • Income legibility matters. A clean Assured Shorthold Tenancy with reliable rent at market rate holds value far better than an informal arrangement or a tenant in arrears.
  • Management burden transfers. Renters' Rights Act 2025 reforms have added compliance weight, so buyers price in future administration costs.

From our experience, here is how the discount typically breaks down:

  • 15–20% discount: regulated AST or Assured Periodic Tenancy, tenant paying market rent, good track record.
  • 20–30% discount: protected tenant, life tenant, or older assured tenant with below-market rent locked in.
  • 30%+ discount: tenant in arrears, damaged property, informal arrangement, or non-standard construction that limits the buyer pool further.

A North East landlord we worked with recently held a three-bed buy to let flat with a regulated tenant paying £380 per month against a market rent of £650. The vacant possession value was £135,000. We offered £108,000, a 20% discount, and completed in 9 days.

Valuing a property with sitting tenants honestly

Valuing a tenanted property starts with the vacant possession figure, then subtracts a tenant risk premium rather than guessing a single number. A sound method:

  • Establish the vacant value through comparable recent sales on the same street or estate.
  • Assess tenancy quality: rent level versus market rent, arrears history, length of occupation, tenant type, and how long the existing tenancy has to run.
  • Apply a discount range: 15% for a strong, compliant tenancy rising to 30% or more for problem tenancies.
  • Stress-test the figure by asking whether a buy to let investor would buy on those terms.

Sellers should treat any single valuation as a starting point, not a final figure, and compare at least one direct cash offer against what an estate agent might quote for the marketing and viewings required to obtain vacant possession.
