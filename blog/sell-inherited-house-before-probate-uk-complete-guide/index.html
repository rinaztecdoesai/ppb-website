Sell Inherited House Before Probate UK: Complete Guide

Can you sell an inherited house before probate UK rules allow? Learn what's legal, what HMRC deadlines apply, and how to sell an inherited home quickly.

Sell Inherited House Before Probate UK: What You Can and Cannot Do

You usually cannot complete the legal sale of an inherited house before probate is granted in the UK, but you can prepare the property, list it, and accept offers in the meantime.

The short answer on sell inherited house before probate UK rules is that preparation is fine, completion is not, until the executor or administrator holds a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration.

Quick answer

You usually cannot complete the legal sale of an inherited house before probate is granted in the UK, but you can prepare the property, list it, and accept offers in the meantime.

Until the executor or administrator holds a Grant of Probate (or Letters of Administration if there is no Will), the property still belongs to the deceased's estate, so a buyer cannot be transferred ownership. What you can do is carry out the work that does not require legal authority: arrange valuations, sort the Will, get the property market-ready, and agree terms with a buyer, provided every contract is marked "subject to probate".

Can I sell an inherited house without probate?

No, you cannot legally complete the sale of an inherited house without probate in the UK. The executor or administrator needs the Grant of Probate to sign a transfer at the Land Registry; without it, no buyer can be registered as the new owner.

You can, however, take many practical steps before the grant arrives:

  • Commission an independent RICS valuation for Inheritance Tax and pricing purposes.
  • File any Inheritance Tax forms with HMRC and apply to HMCTS for the grant.
  • List the property, conduct viewings, and accept a written offer, clearly marked as subject to probate.
  • Get quotes from solicitors and surveyors so you are ready to instruct them the day the grant lands.

What is the 6 month rule for probate?

The 6 month rule is an HMRC deadline: Inheritance Tax must be paid within six months of the end of the month in which the deceased died, or interest starts accruing on the unpaid amount.

For example, if someone dies on 14 March 2025, the IHT bill is due by 30 September 2025. Selling a property before that deadline is often the executor's quickest way to raise the cash, which is why many executors ask whether they can sell inherited house before probate UK rules permit it. The answer is that contracts cannot exchange before the grant, but a cash buyer with a fixed offer can complete within days of it being issued.

Can I sell my dad's house while waiting for probate?

You can market and even exchange contracts on your dad's house while waiting for probate, but completion cannot happen until the Grant of Probate is issued and you can sign the Land Registry transfer.

If there is a Will naming you as executor, you have authority in principle. If there is no Will, a beneficiary must first apply for Letters of Administration. In both cases, many buyers will proceed on a "subject to probate" basis, especially cash buyers like Prime Property Buyers, who routinely exchange before the grant and complete in as little as 5 days once it lands.

Can I empty a house before probate is granted?

You can clear and remove items from a house before probate, but doing so without proper records exposes the executor to accusations of mishandling estate assets. Photograph every room, log every item removed, store valuables securely, and keep receipts for any disposal costs; these records protect the executor and the beneficiaries.

Can I Sell an Inherited House Without Probate?

What "subject to probate" actually means for marketing a property

You cannot legally sell an inherited house before probate is granted in England. The property sits in the deceased's estate, so no executor, administrator, or beneficiary has the legal authority to transfer title to a buyer. Contracts signed without that authority can be void, leaving the sale to collapse and exposing the executor to personal liability.

What you can do is prepare and market the property while the application is in progress. Estate agents and cash buyers routinely list inherited homes as "subject to probate" or "subject to obtaining the Grant of Probate". That wording tells every viewer that the sale can only complete once the legal right to sell exists, and it keeps your marketing compliant and accurate.

Three practical steps you can take now:

  • Appoint an estate agent or direct cash buyer, such as Prime Property Buyers, to value the home and confirm realistic timescales.
  • Commission a probate valuation from a RICS surveyor so the figure you list aligns with the Inheritance Tax form you will file.
  • Gather identification documents, the death certificate, and the will (if one exists) so the eventual Grant of Probate application is not held up.

Preparation is not the same as a legally binding sale, but it shortens the gap between grant and completion once the Grant of Probate lands.

Why the Grant of Probate protects executors and beneficiaries

The Grant of Probate gives the executor formal authority to deal with the estate. Until the grant is issued, even a well-meaning sale carries risk. If the deceased left a will, probate confirms who the executor is; if there is no will, the court issues Letters of Administration to an administrator, and the Rules of Intestacy govern who inherits.

The Grant also protects you as a personal representative. A buyer who parts with the deposit before grant has no assurance that you hold authority. Solicitors acting for that buyer usually insist on seeing the grant before they release funds on completion. The Land Registry will not register a change of ownership without evidence that the seller had the right to sell, and HMRC will not accept any Inheritance Tax calculation without a probate valuation. Skipping the grant therefore creates problems on three fronts at once: title, tax, and personal liability.

Beneficiaries gain from probate because it locks in their entitlement. Without the grant, no assent can be lodged with the Land Registry to transfer the property into a beneficiary's name, and no cash distribution can be made.

Get the grant first, then sell. Cash buyers who specialise in probate, like Prime Property Buyers, can complete in as little as five days once the grant is in hand, so the delay rarely costs you much time in the long run.

What Is the 6-Month Rule for Probate?

The 6-month rule for probate is the deadline by which executors must pay any Inheritance Tax due on a deceased person's estate to avoid interest charges. HMRC requires IHT payments within six months of the end of the month in which the person died, and the Grant of Probate is only issued once this is settled.

How Inheritance Tax deadlines shape the executor's timeline

When someone dies, their executor must value the entire estate, including any property, savings, and possessions, and submit an IHT400 form to HMRC if Inheritance Tax applies. The current Nil Rate Band is £325,000, with the Residence Nil Rate Band adding up to £175,000 when a direct descendant inherits the family home. Anything above these allowances is taxed at 40%.

That tax bill must reach HMRC within six months of the end of the month of death, or interest starts accruing daily. For estates where the property alone exceeds the threshold, this creates real pressure. Many executors need the proceeds of a house sale to actually pay the IHT, but they cannot legally complete a sale until probate is granted. This timing trap forces families to choose between taking a bridging loan, selling quickly at a discount, or waiting and accepting interest penalties.

In our experience buying probate properties since 1993, the most common reason executors contact us is exactly this: they owe IHT, they cannot complete a sale, and the six-month deadline is approaching. A cash buyer who funds purchases directly can break that deadlock by agreeing a fixed offer, covering all legal fees, and completing in as little as 5 days once probate comes through, giving the executor certainty on price and timing.

Practical options if the deadline is tight

If you are executor and the IHT clock is running, you have a few realistic paths:

  • Apply for probate immediately using a solicitor or the online probate service. Straightforward estates currently take 4–8 weeks once the application is submitted; complex ones can take much longer.
  • Pay IHT from other estate funds (savings, ISAs, investments) to stop the interest clock, then sell the property at full market value once probate is granted.
  • Request a payment plan from HMRC if the estate genuinely cannot pay on time. This stops the daily interest accrual.
  • Sell to a cash buyer before probate completes, with the contract marked "subject to grant of probate". The buyer commits, the executor gets a guaranteed completion date, and funds release the moment probate is issued.

The critical thing to know: an executor can accept an offer and even exchange contracts before probate, but cannot transfer legal title or complete the sale until the Grant of Probate is in hand. Any buyer or solicitor who tells you otherwise is either mistaken or cutting corners.

Can I Sell My Dad's House While Waiting for Probate?

You cannot legally exchange contracts or complete the sale of your dad's house before probate is granted, but you can prepare the property for sale and accept an offer in the meantime. Any agreement must be marked "subject to probate" so all parties understand the sale cannot legally conclude until the executor holds formal authority.

What you can do before probate arrives

Executors in England are entitled to take practical steps that move the sale forward while the Grant of Probate is being processed. The most common actions include:

  • Obtaining professional valuations from estate agents and, where required, RICS surveyors.
  • Commissioning an Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) and preparing the property for viewings.
  • Instructing a solicitor or licensed conveyancer to draft the contract pack.
  • Marketing the property, either on the open market or to a direct cash buyer like Prime Property Buyers.
  • Accepting a written offer, provided the buyer understands completion cannot take place yet.

None of these steps transfer ownership. They position the estate to complete within days of the Grant of Probate being issued, which matters because Inheritance Tax payments and beneficiary distributions depend on liquidating the property efficiently.

What you cannot legally do before probate

The executor cannot sign a transfer deed, receive purchase monies into the estate account, or hand keys to a buyer until the Grant of Probate has been issued. A buyer who exchanges contracts before probate risks the deal collapsing if a beneficiary later disputes the Will, if HMRC raises a query on the IHT-400, or if HMCTS delays the application. In practice, we have seen sales agreed in good faith fall through simply because the grant took longer than expected.

Why timing matters for the estate

Executors should consider three deadlines when deciding how early to prepare a sale:

  • Inheritance Tax due date: Six months from the end of the month of death. Selling before this point helps the executor avoid interest charges from HMRC.
  • Probate processing time: Straightforward estates typically take 8 to 12 weeks; complex estates can run 12 to 18 months.
  • Beneficiary pressure: Beneficiaries waiting on sale proceeds to pay off mortgages or release equity can apply real pressure on the executor's timeline.

Accepting offers and preparing contracts before the grant arrives

Once a credible offer is on the table, instruct your solicitor to issue draft contracts and carry out local authority searches. This pre-work shortens the gap between grant and completion from weeks to days. Emptying a house before probate: what is and isn't allowed

You can clear perishable items, arrange rubbish removal, and prepare the property for viewings, but should not dispose of furniture, personal effects, or valuables until probate is granted. Executors who remove or sell estate assets before receiving the grant risk accusations of misappropriation from beneficiaries and may be held personally liable. Where the home contains items of clear value, photograph and log them, and wait for the grant before distributing anything.

Exceptions, Ownership Structures and Tax Traps to Understand

Joint ownership, trusts and other ways property can skip probate

Not every inherited house must pass through probate. If the deceased owned the property as joint tenants with a surviving owner, the surviving owner automatically inherits the whole share through a process called survivorship. The Land Registry records the transfer with a simple form DJP, and probate is not required to sell.

Tenants in common ownership works differently. Each owner holds a defined share, and that share forms part of the estate. You will need a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration before that share can be sold or transferred, even if the surviving co-owner wants to keep the property.

Property held in a living trust also sits outside probate. The trustees named in the trust document can sell once the settlor has died, provided the trust deed gives them that power. Many people set up trusts deliberately to spare their family the delay of a probate application.

Where there is no Will, the Rules of Intestacy decide who inherits. An Administrator, rather than an Executor, applies for Letters of Administration to gain authority. The legal standing is similar, but the application route and paperwork differ.

Inheritance Tax, Capital Gains Tax and the executor's year

Inheritance Tax (IHT) is due if the estate exceeds £325,000, with the Residence Nil Rate Band adding up to £175,000 more when a home passes to direct descendants. HMRC must receive payment within six months of the end of the month in which the person died. Selling an asset within that window is a common way executors raise funds to settle the bill.

Capital Gains Tax is rarely triggered on a probate sale itself, because the property's value on the date of death becomes the new base cost. It can arise later if a beneficiary who is not a spouse sells, or if the executor sells at a later valuation.

Executors are expected to distribute the estate within the executor's year (12 months from the date of death). Beneficiaries can ask for reasonable interest if this window is missed without good reason, which keeps pressure on delays.
